The Project Management Institute (PMI) provides a Practice Standard for Project Risk Management with the publication of this document. It is designed to assist project managers in implementing risk management processes and practices.

Here are the key points regarding risk management from the text:

1. The practice standard outlines a structured and proactive approach to risk management, which includes identifying, analyzing, responding to, and monitoring project risks.
2. The process begins with risk identification, which involves determining potential risks that may affect the project's objectives. This can be done through various techniques such as brainstorming, interviewing stakeholders, or using checklists.
3. Once risks are identified, they are analyzed to determine their probability of occurrence and the impact they would have on the project. This information is used to prioritize risks and determine the appropriate level of response.
4. Risk responses can include avoiding the risk, transferring it to another party, mitigating its impact, or accepting it. The chosen response will depend on the nature of the risk, its potential impact, and the organization's risk tolerance.
5. Risk monitoring and control involves tracking identified risks throughout the project's life cycle and adjusting risk responses as necessary. This includes updating the risk management plan, communicating risk information to stakeholders, and documenting lessons learned.
6. The practice standard also emphasizes the importance of integrating risk management into the overall project management process, including planning, executing, and monitoring and controlling.
7. The text mentions that the PMI's Risk Management Professional (PMI-RMP) certification is designed for those who wish to demonstrate their expertise in risk management.

The key concepts of Project Risk Management, as described in the Practice Standard for Project Risk Management, include:

* Project risk is defined as an uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on a project's objectives.
* The definition of project risk includes two key dimensions: uncertainty and effect on a project's objectives.
* When assessing the importance of a project risk, both the uncertainty and the effect must be considered.
* The uncertainty dimension can be described using the term "probability" and the effect can be called "impact".
* Project risk includes both distinct events which are uncertain but can be clearly described, and more general conditions which are less specific but also may give rise to uncertainty.
* Project risk encompasses uncertain events which could have a negative effect on a project's objectives, as well as those which could have a positive effect. These two types of risk are called, respectively, threats and opportunities.
Risks are uncertain future events or conditions that may or may not occur, but would matter if they did. When a risk event occurs, it ceases to be uncertain. Threats which occur may be called issues or problems, while opportunities which occur may be called benefits. Both issues/problems and benefits entail project management actions that are outside the scope of the Project Risk Management process.

It is useful to consider project risk at two levels: individual risks and overall project risk. Individual risks are specific events or conditions that might affect project objectives and can be positive or negative, affecting one or more project objectives, elements, or tasks. Understanding individual risks can assist in determining how to apply effort and resources to enhance the chances of project success. Day-to-day Project Risk Management focuses on these individual risks to enhance the prospects of a successful project outcome. Overall project risk represents the effect of uncertainty on project objectives and can be influenced by individual risks.

The Identify Risks process is the first step in project risk management, with the purpose of identifying all knowable risks to project objectives. The process should be iterative, repeating to find new risks that have become knowable since the previous iteration. Risk identification should be performed as early as possible in the project lifecycle, even with high uncertainty and less information. Early risk identification enables key project decisions to take maximum account of project risks. Critical success factors for the Identify Risks process include early identification and potential response identification, which should be recorded and considered for immediate action if appropriate.
Risk identification is an iterative process that should be carried out throughout the project life cycle, including at key milestones or during significant changes to the project or its environment. Risks can be identified at any time, not just during formal identification events or reviews. A broad range of sources and perspectives should be considered to ensure that all potential uncertainties are identified. Opportunities should also be explicitly identified during the risk identification process. Each identified risk should be linked to at least one project objective, such as time, cost, quality, or scope. Early identification and response to risks is efficient as it can minimize costs.

* Identifying risks should be done per project objective, considering that some risks may affect multiple objectives.
* Risk statements should be complete and unambiguous, describing the uncertainty and its causes and effects in detail.
* Risks should be described at a level of detail that allows assignment to a single owner with clear responsibility and accountability. Trigger conditions should also be identified if possible.
* Objectivity during the risk identification process is important to minimize subjectivity and allow for the identification of as many risks as possible. Sources of bias should be managed proactively.
* A range of tools and techniques is available for the risk identification process.

Risk identification falls into three categories:

1. Historical Review:
	* Based on past projects, either within the same organization or in other organizations
	* Relies on comparison to similar projects and careful data selection
	* Involves considering risks from the selected historical situations and assessing if they could arise in the current project
2. Current Assessments:
	* Rely on detailed analysis of the current project
	* Analyze project characteristics against given frameworks and models to expose areas of uncertainty
	* Does not rely on outside reference points, purely based on examination of the project
3. Creativity Techniques:
	* Encourages project stakeholders to use imagination to find risks
	* Can be used singly or in groups, with varying degrees of structure
	* Depends on the ability of participants to think creatively
	* Success is enhanced by use of a skilled facilitator

No single technique can reveal all knowable risks, and the Identify Risks process should use a combination of techniques.
Relevant information about risk management:

* A combination of risk identification techniques can be used, such as a risk identification checklist (historical review), assumptions analysis (current assessment), and brainstorming (creativity).
* Use of a risk breakdown structure, prompt list, or set of generic list categories can assist in identifying potential risks.
* Identified risks should be unambiguously described using structured risk descriptions and risk meta-language, which distinguishes a risk from its cause(s) and effect(s) using three-part statements in the form: “As a result of cause, risk may occur, which would lead to effect.”
* The results from the risk identification process should be recorded in a risk register, which includes a properly structured risk description, the nominated risk owner for each risk, and may also include information on the causes and effects of the risk, trigger conditions, and preliminary responses.

The Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis process:

- Assesses and evaluates characteristics of individually identified project risks.
- Prioritizes risks based on agreed-upon characteristics.
- Evaluates the probability of each risk and the effect of each risk on project objectives.
- Categorizes risks according to their sources or causes to identify common root causes and more effective risk responses.
- Identifies common effects from groups of risks to identify areas of greatest risk exposure (e.g. project completion date, budget, deliverable scope).
- Provides project management with the characteristics of risks that have the most influence on achieving the project's objectives.
- High-priority risks, whether threatening or enhancing the achievement of project objectives, are an important focus in the Plan Risk Responses process and may be further analyzed in the Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process.
Relevant information about risk management:

- Successful qualitative risk analysis is based on an agreed-upon approach for assessing all identified risks in a project.
- Risks are assessed according to probability of occurrence and impact on individual objectives.
- Other factors considered in determining the importance of a risk include:
	+ Urgency (proximity), indicated by lead time necessary to execute a risk response and clarity of symptoms/warning signs.
	+ Manageability, with options to go forward, stop/re-scope the project, inform the customer, or address risks that affect the enterprise beyond the project.

Risk assessment should be based on agreed-upon definitions of terms and use these definitions consistently. High-quality information about risks should be collected, which may require interviews, workshops, and expert judgment. Data gathered may be subject to bias, which should be identified and remedied if possible. Iterative qualitative risk analysis should be performed periodically throughout the project, with the frequency determined during the Plan Risk Management process. The Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis Process involves tools and techniques to assess individual risks and identify those important to the project's success.
* Qualitative risk analysis tools help prioritize risks based on their characteristics, such as probability of occurrence and impact on objectives.
* Output from these tools is a list of risks prioritized in order of importance or in priority groups (e.g. high, moderate, low).
* Data collection and evaluation is crucial for individual risk assessment, using tools such as interviews, workshops, and references to databases of prior projects.
* It is important to protect against bias in data gathering, especially when relying on expert judgment.
* Some tools allow prioritizing risks based on their impact on specific objectives, providing a list of risks important for any specific objective of interest to management.
* Prioritizing risks based on their impact on the entire project can be useful for communication with management and other stakeholders. A single risk prioritization index can be constructed for this purpose.
The Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis process should involve categorizing risks, which can lead to improved analysis of risk probability and magnitude, as well as effective responses. Identifying common root causes of risks can reveal the magnitude of risk events and effective strategies for addressing multiple risks simultaneously. Understanding the chain of risks can lead to a better understanding of risk implications for the project. Identifying risks that can occur at the same time or using the same resources for recovery can provide a realistic picture of risk mitigation using scarce resources.

Combining the results of the Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis process with the risk breakdown structure can show clusters of priority risks arising from specific sources. Assessing the impact of high-priority risks on specific objectives, such as the schedule, can indicate which activities to address to reduce uncertainty. Documenting the results of the qualitative risk analysis is necessary.
The Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis process:

* Prioritizes risks based on probability of occurring and potential impact on project objectives or the whole project.
* Each identified risk is assigned a priority.
* Information is stored in a risk register, which can be easily updated with new information.
* High priority risks are segregated for further analysis and response planning, and are generally monitored frequently.
* Risks of low priority to the project may be placed on a watch list and are reviewed less often for changes in their status.
The Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process provides a numerical estimate of the overall effect of risk on project objectives, considering risks simultaneously. It can be used to evaluate the likelihood of success in achieving project objectives and to estimate contingency reserves for time and cost. Quantitative risk analysis, such as Monte Carlo simulation, may provide more realism in the estimate of overall project cost or schedule than a non-probabilistic approach. However, it is not always required or appropriate for all projects and may require more effort than qualitative risk analysis. The Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process calculates estimates of overall project risk, usually at a detailed level such as the line-item cost or schedule activity level.
- Overall risk analysis using quantitative methods estimates the implication of all quantified risks on project objectives.
- Implementation requires:
	+ Complete and accurate representation of project objectives, such as the project schedule or cost estimate.
	+ Identifying risks on individual project elements like schedule activities or line-item costs.
	+ Including generic risks that have a broader effect than individual project elements.
	+ Applying a quantitative method (e.g., Monte Carlo simulation or decision tree analysis) that incorporates multiple risks simultaneously.
- Quantitative analysis results will be compared to the project plan to:
	+ Estimate the overall project risk.
	+ Answer questions like the probability of meeting the project's objectives, contingency reserve needed, and parts of the project contributing the most risk.
	+ Identify individual risks contributing the most to overall project risk.
- Quantitative methods help distinguish projects where quantified risks require more attention.
The Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process is dependent on the following critical success factors:

1. Prior Risk Identification and Qualitative Risk Analysis: The Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process should consider all significant risks identified during the Identify Risks and Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis processes.
2. Appropriate Project Model: An appropriate model of the project should be used as the basis for quantitative risk analysis, using project schedules for time, line-item cost estimates for cost, decision trees for decisions in the face of uncertainty, and other total-project models.
3. Commitment to Collecting High-Quality Risk Data: High-quality risk data may not be available in historic databases and should be gathered using expert judgment of those present, through interviews, workshops, and other means.

Quantitative risk analysis is used to threaten objectives beyond the tolerance of the stakeholders, with vigorous risk responses aimed at protecting the most important objectives. The Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process occurs after the Identify Risks and Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis processes have been completed, using a prioritized list of identified risks. Quantitative risk analysis is sensitive to the completeness and correctness of the project model used.
Success in gathering risk analysis data requires recognition and combat of biases such as cognitive and motivational bias. The Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis process derives overall project risk from individual risks using appropriate methods such as Monte Carlo simulation for cost and schedule, and decision trees for uncertain future events. Risks are specified at the level of detailed tasks or line-item costs and incorporated into a project model to calculate effects on objectives like schedule or cost. Interrelationships between risks should be addressed by correlating risks with common root causes or listing risks in a risk register and attaching it to several project elements.
Relevant information about risk management:

* Risk models can represent many risks that impact project objectives simultaneously, including both opportunities and threats.
* Quantitative models calculate the effect of risks on project objectives, typically identified and quantified at a level of detail below the total project.
* Probability models are used to address uncertainty, represented predominantly as probability of an event's occurring or as probability distributions for a range of outcomes.
* Monte Carlo simulation tools can combine probability distributions of line-item costs or schedule activity durations.
* Data gathering tools include assessment of historical data, workshops, interviews, or questionnaires to gather quantified information on the probability of a risk occurring, its potential impacts on cost or time, or relationships such as correlation between risks.
* Quantitative analysis results are not typically available in standard deterministic project management methods.

Contingency reserves may also be used for capturing project opportunities. The urgency of risk response may depend on the probability of achieving the plan's objectives or the amount of contingency reserve required.

The Plan Risk Responses process determines effective response actions for individual risks and the overall project risk, taking into account stakeholders' risk attitudes, conventions specified in the Risk Management Plan, and any constraints and assumptions. The objective of the Plan Risk Responses process is to determine the set of actions that enhances the chances of project success while complying with applicable organizational and project constraints. Plans should be developed for addressing every risk the project team considers to be sufficiently important.
Relevant information about risk management:

* Risk response planning involves agreeing on actions to be taken and potential changes to budget, schedule, resources, and scope.
* Contingent risk response actions should be executed at the optimum time, with trigger conditions described.
* Responsibility for monitoring project conditions and implementing corresponding actions should be clearly assigned.
* Each risk should be allocated to a risk owner and each risk response to a risk action owner.
* Risk owners are responsible for ensuring the risk response is effective and planning additional risk responses if required.
* Risk action owners are responsible for ensuring agreed-upon risk responses are carried out as planned, in a timely manner.
* Roles of risk owner and risk action owner may be assigned to a single person.
* Responses, when implemented, can generate additional risks (secondary risks), which need to be analyzed and planned for.
* It is not feasible or desirable to eliminate all threats from a project; residual risks will remain after responses have been implemented.
* Residual risks should be clearly identified, analyzed, documented, and communicated to all relevant stakeholders.
* All approved, unconditional actions arising from risk response planning should be integrated into the project.
Relevant information about risk management from the section includes:

* Risk response processes should be carried out as part of normal project implementation, along with project change management, configuration control, project planning, budgeting, scheduling, resource management, and project communication planning.
* Critical success factors for the Plan Risk Responses process include:
	+ Communication: maintaining open and appropriate communication with stakeholders, disseminating plans, obtaining approval, and addressing organizational causes of risks.
	+ Clearly defining risk-related roles and responsibilities: assigning a single risk owner and a single risk action owner for each identified risk and agreed-upon risk response.
* Risk owners and risk action owners should be aware of their responsibilities and other stakeholders should understand and accept their needs and authority.

* Risk-related contingency reserves should be approved and tracked by management.
* Risk responses should be integrated into the project management plan, scheduled, and assigned for execution.

* Management should support the project manager in developing risk responses and authorizing corresponding resources.
* Responses may be developed to address risks related by cause and effect or by common root cause.
* Consideration of the interaction of risks and responses is needed, as one risk may affect the probability or impact of other risks.
* A compromise may be required in deciding on the response strategy, as some proposed responses may be mutually exclusive or counterproductive.

* The goal of planning responses to risks is to control the potential effects of the risk treatment strategy.
* Risk response plans should be appropriate, timely, cost-effective, feasible, achievable, agreed-upon, assigned, and accepted.
* Proposed risk response plans should be assessed for consistency with organizational values, project objectives, and stakeholder expectations; technical feasibility; and the ability of the project team or risk action owners to carry out the corresponding actions.
* Risk response planning should balance the overall impact of the response on project objectives and the improvement in the risk profile of the project.
* Risk response planning should address both threats and opportunities, and should be carried out at a general, strategic level before developing detailed tactical approaches.
* Strategic risk responses should be integrated into the project management plan, including the schedule, budget, and resources.
The relevant information about risk management from the section includes:

1. Risk response strategies should be developed for individual risks, sets of risks, and project-level risks, with the involvement of affected stakeholders.
2. There are four risk response strategies for threats and opportunities:
	* Avoid a Threat or Exploit an Opportunity: This strategy involves taking actions to ensure that the threat cannot occur or can have no effect on the project, or that the opportunity will occur and the project will be able to take advantage of it.
	* Transfer a Threat or Share an Opportunity: This strategy entails transference to a third party that is better positioned to address a particular threat or opportunity.
	* Mitigate a Threat or Enhance an Opportunity: This strategy aims to identify actions that will decrease the probability and/or the impact of a threat, and increase the probability and/or the impact of an opportunity.
	* Accept a Threat or an Opportunity: This strategy applies when the other strategies are not considered applicable or feasible. Acceptance entails taking no action unless the risk actually occurs, in which case contingency or fallback plans may be developed ahead of time.
3. These strategies need to be agreed upon by the entity that approves those strategies.
Four risk response strategies for overall project risk are:

1. Cancel the project if the level of risk remains unacceptable.
2. Share the risk with the customer and supplier in a business structure.
3. Re-plan the project, for example by modifying project priority, resource allocations, delivery calendar.
4. Pursue the project despite a risk exposure that exceeds the desired level.

- Risk management involves identifying risks, performing quantitative risk analysis, and planning risk responses. This process is repeated until individual risks are acceptable and overall risk is within a predefined threshold.

The Monitor and Control Risks process aims to:

- Track identified risks and monitor residual risks
- Identify new risks
- Ensure that risk response plans are executed at the appropriate time and evaluated for effectiveness throughout the project life cycle
- Review the effectiveness of all Project Risk Management processes for current and future projects
- Monitor trigger conditions for contingent responses and ensure timely execution of corresponding actions
- Include and define all approved unconditional response actions in the current project management plan and take appropriate action if necessary

The action owner is responsible for monitoring trigger conditions and executing corresponding actions in a timely manner. The first action of risk monitoring and controlling is to ensure that all agreed-upon actions are included and defined in the current project management plan.
The risk owners and risk action owners need to be informed of any changes that affect their responsibilities. Regular communication between them and the project manager is necessary to ensure that stakeholders accept accountability for controlling specific risks, track associated trigger conditions, and carry out agreed-upon responses in a timely manner. The Plan Risk Responses process includes a mechanism for measuring the effectiveness of responses. Risk reassessment should be performed periodically or in response to project events, including the occurrence of major or unexpected risks, analysis of complex change requests, phase end reviews, project re-planning or major plan elaboration, and periodic reviews to ensure information remains current. In the event of major organizational changes, risk management planning may need to be revisited prior to reassessing risks. Regular audits should be performed to determine strengths and weaknesses in risk management.
Relevant information about risk management:

- An integrated analysis of the risk management process should be conducted at the end of the project, focusing on long-term process improvements.

- At project closure, the project manager should ensure that a description has been given of the closure of every risk in the risk register, including whether it did not occur, occurred and contingency plan was invoked, or occurred and had an impact on the project scope (time, cost, and quality).
- The output of the audit of the risk management process should be consolidated with specific information with respect to the project's experience of risks.
- Any generally applicable guidelines for the organization should be highlighted and potential actions proposed for applying them, which can lead to an update to the corresponding organizational process assets.

Risk management can be based on assumptions and constraints listed in the project charter. It is important to generate a comprehensive list of assumptions and constraints to identify project-specific risks. Brainstorming can be used to generate ideas and involve key stakeholders, but it may be difficult to arrange and expensive. It can also be prone to groupthink and biased results if dominated by a strong person. Good facilitation, attendance of a representative group of stakeholders, and commitment to honesty are necessary for effective brainstorming.

Cause and effect (Ishikawa) diagrams can promote structured thinking and effectively select critical impacts through the use of sensitivity analysis. However, the diagram can quickly become over-complex.

Checklists can capture previous experience and present a detailed list of risks, but they can also become unwieldy and may only include threats, missing opportunities. Regular maintenance and use of structure, such as a risk breakdown structure (RBS), can assist in effective use of checklists.

The Delphi technique can capture input from technical experts and remove sources of bias, but it is limited to technical risks and dependent on the actual expertise of experts. It may also take a longer time than available due to iterations of experts' inputs. Effective facilitation and clear definition of scope are necessary for the Delphi technique. It can expose detailed project-specific risks and requires no specialist tools, but it is limited to risks contained in the project.

In root-cause analysis, the probability of a risk occurring can be specified in several different ways, one common way is to assign levels of risk probability by ranges of probability. This approach allows subject matter experts to assess a risk's probability within a range rather than as a specific value. For example, in a 5x5 Probability and Impact Matrix, probability ranges can be more tightly defined at low levels of probability. The levels of impact definitions are very project-specific and the values used to specify the level of impact from very low to very high should be higher as they move from very low to very high for a specific objective. The levels are defined by the organization as causing the same amount of pain or gain to the project for each level across objectives. If a risk's possible impact is uncertain and could be assigned to more than one level, the organization should define how to handle such cases.

Risk management involves the assessment of the probability and impact of uncertain events. Expected Monetary Value (EMV) is a simple calculation used to determine the expected value of uncertain events. However, EMV only provides the expected value of uncertain events and may not provide sufficient information for risk decisions. In situations where more information is needed, Monte Carlo simulation may be a more appropriate tool as it allows all specified risks to vary simultaneously and provides quantitative estimates of overall project risk. Monte Carlo simulation can answer questions such as the likelihood of the base plan to be successful, the amount of contingency in time and cost needed to achieve a desired level of confidence, and which activities are important in determining the overall project risk.

To effectively apply EMV and Monte Carlo simulation, it is important to identify all possible events that need to be included in the calculation, have access to historical data or expert opinions on the values of probability and impact, and understand the difference between EMV and the output of simulation tools. The quality of the input data for Monte Carlo simulation depends heavily on the expert judgment and the effort and expertise of the risk analyst. Monte Carlo simulation may be resisted by management as being unnecessary or too sophisticated compared to traditional project management tools.

• The OGC's Management of Risk guidance defines risk as "the chance of something happening that will have an impact on objectives" and risk management as "the process by which risks are identified, assessed and controlled."
• Both the PMBOK® Guide and the OGC's Management of Risk guidance emphasize the importance of risk management in achieving project objectives and achieving a desired outcome.

Risk management is a critical aspect of project management, and it includes the following components:

1. Risk identification: This involves the comprehensive identification of risks, which can be emergent or early identified. High-quality risk data is essential for this process, and it can be collected through data gathering tools, historical review, and individual risk plans.
2. Risk analysis: There are two types of risk analysis - qualitative and quantitative. Qualitative risk analysis involves the use of creativity techniques, impact analysis, and an iterative process to assess risks. Quantitative risk analysis, on the other hand, uses data and statistical methods to determine the probability and impact of risks. Techniques used in quantitative risk analysis include decision tree analysis and Monte Carlo analysis.
3. Risk management plan: A risk management plan is essential for effective risk management. It includes the management of constraints, contingency plan, contingency reserve plan, and risk responses. The plan should also cover critical success factors, individual risk plans, and overall project risk plans.
4. Risk monitoring and control: This involves monitoring risks throughout the project and controlling them through appropriate responses. Risk monitoring and control should be based on high-quality information, and it includes risk monitoring, tracking, and documentation of results.
5. Risk responses: There are various risk responses, including contingency response strategies, objective plan risk responses, and critical success factor plan risk responses. These responses should be designed to address both threats and opportunities.
